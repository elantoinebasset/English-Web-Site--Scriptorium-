How to Recognize It?

Distinctive Features of Korean Script
  • Hangul: The Korean alphabet, created in the 15th century, is logical, phonetic, and unique to Korea.
  • Block Formation: Characters are grouped into syllabic blocks (e.g., 한, 국, 어), each representing one syllable.
  • Consonants and Vowels: Built from 14 basic consonants and 10 vowels, often combined to form complex letters.
  • No Chinese Characters Needed: Unlike Japanese, modern Korean rarely uses Chinese characters (Hanja).
  • Romanization: Korean can also be written using the Latin alphabet for learners or international use.

Writing Basics

Hangul, the Korean script, was invented by King Sejong in 1443 to promote literacy. It is praised for its scientific design, ease of learning, and clarity.

Basic Principles
  • Written left to right, top to bottom
  • Each character block contains a consonant-vowel (CV) structure, optionally ending with another consonant (CVC)
  • Logical shapes: consonants mimic the shape of the mouth/tongue when spoken
  • Invented script: not borrowed, uniquely Korean
  • Few irregularities, consistent pronunciation
  • Used in both North and South Korea (with slight vocabulary and spelling differences)

History

Origins and Evolution

Before Hangul, Korean was written using Chinese characters (Hanja), making literacy difficult. Hangul was invented to provide a script everyone could learn and use easily.

Timeline of Development
  • 1443: King Sejong commissions the creation of Hangul
  • 1446: Publication of the script as "Hunminjeongeum" (Correct Sounds for the Instruction of the People)
  • Late 1800s: Hangul gains widespread use in newspapers and education
  • 20th century: Official standardization and removal of Hanja in most writing
  • Today: Hangul is used exclusively in everyday life across both Koreas
Fun Facts
  • Hangul has been praised by linguists as one of the most scientific writing systems
  • October 9 is celebrated as Hangul Day in South Korea
  • There’s a monument dedicated to King Sejong in Seoul for creating the script
  • The shape of consonants shows how the mouth is positioned when pronounced
  • Some North Korean words and spellings differ slightly from the South
