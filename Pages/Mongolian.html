How to Recognize It?

Distinctive Features of Mongolian Script
  • Vertical Script: Traditional Mongolian is written vertically from top to bottom and columns go left to right.
  • Unique Appearance: The script features elegant, flowing characters often connected within syllables.
  • Alphabet-Based: Derived from the Old Uyghur script, with adaptations to fit Mongolian phonetics.
  • Multiple Systems: Mongolia uses both Cyrillic (official) and traditional Mongolian scripts, while Inner Mongolia (China) primarily uses the traditional one.
  • Romanization: Like Korean, Mongolian can also be written using the Latin alphabet for learning and international use.

Writing Basics

The Mongolian script is one of the few vertical scripts still in use today. It originated in the 13th century and has been preserved through cultural pride and revival efforts.

Basic Principles
  • Written top to bottom, columns from left to right
  • Characters change shape depending on their position in a word (initial, medial, final)
  • Elegant strokes and curves; letters connect fluidly
  • Cyrillic script is also used in modern Mongolia (since 1940s)
  • Traditional script is being revitalized in education and official settings
  • Used for Mongolian, Manchu, and even Buddhist texts historically

History

Origins and Evolution

The traditional Mongolian script was developed in the early 1200s by adapting the Old Uyghur alphabet. It became the official writing system of the Mongol Empire and was used for centuries.

Timeline of Development
  • 1204: Adoption of the script by Genghis Khan from the Uyghur script
  • 1600s: Used widely in administrative and religious texts across the empire
  • 1940s: Mongolia adopts Cyrillic under Soviet influence
  • 1990s–Today: Efforts to reintroduce and teach traditional script in Mongolia
  • Now: Used officially in Inner Mongolia (China) and increasingly in Mongolia itself
Fun Facts
  • One of the only vertical alphabetic scripts still used today
  • Characters visually resemble calligraphy or brush strokes
  • The script is encoded in Unicode and supported on modern devices
  • Revival efforts include street signs, school curricula, and public documents
  • Some letters resemble different shapes depending on their word position
