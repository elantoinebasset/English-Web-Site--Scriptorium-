Hebrew Writing System

כתב שלום

(Ktav Shalom – "Writing Peace")

How to Recognize It?

Distinctive Features of the Hebrew Alphabet
  • Right to Left: Hebrew is written from right to left.
  • 22 Letters: Only consonants are represented in the main script.
  • No Uppercase: All letters have a single form; there’s no case distinction.
  • Square Script: Modern Hebrew uses a blocky, square-shaped script.
  • Vowel Markings: Optional diacritical marks (nikkud) indicate vowels, mostly for children or religious texts.
  • Special Final Forms: Five letters change form when appearing at the end of a word.

Writing Basics

The Hebrew script, used since the first millennium BCE, is a consonant-based writing system originally used for ancient Hebrew and now for modern Hebrew, Yiddish, and other Jewish languages. It's a key element of Jewish identity and tradition.

Basic Principles
  • Written from right to left
  • 22 consonantal letters
  • No uppercase/lowercase distinction
  • Five final letter forms (sofit)
  • Vowel markings used in educational and religious contexts
  • No punctuation in ancient texts; modern Hebrew uses standard punctuation

History

Origins and Evolution

The Hebrew alphabet evolved from the Phoenician script and has undergone several transformations—from Paleo-Hebrew to the modern "Ashuri" script used today. It is deeply tied to Jewish liturgical, cultural, and national identity.

Timeline of Development
  • 10th century BCE: Earliest known Hebrew inscriptions (Paleo-Hebrew)
  • 6th century BCE: Adoption of Aramaic script during Babylonian exile
  • 2nd century CE: Formalization of square Hebrew script
  • Middle Ages: Use in Jewish scholarship and codices, vocalization developed
  • 20th century: Revival of Hebrew as a spoken and written language
Fun Facts
  • Hebrew is one of the few ancient languages revived for modern use.
  • It has no written vowels in normal texts—readers infer them from context.
  • The Torah, Jewish holy scripture, is written in Hebrew on parchment scrolls.
  • Used in Jewish religious rituals, academic study, and Israeli society.
  • Yiddish also uses Hebrew script with slight modifications.
  • Modern Hebrew evolved alongside the Zionist movement in the 19th–20th centuries.
